The Hormuz
break-even line

When the strait shut, every Gulf exporter lost barrels. Only some lost money. Saudi Arabia is the largest exporter through Hormuz and its oil revenue rose. The UAE is the third largest and its revenue fell. Production scale explains nothing about who survived. One variable, fixed years before the war, explains almost all of it.

The chokepoint, to scale

Fourteen million barrels a day, one aperture

Crude and condensate from seven exporters converge on a four-mile-wide shipping lane and fan out to Asia. Two countries own pipelines that reach open water without using it. Everyone else has no route out of the Gulf at all. Switch the state below to see what the closure physically did to the flow.

The model

Revenue is volume times price. Price did the rescuing.

Brent ran at 1.59× its pre-war level through the conflict window. That multiple sets a single threshold the whole market shares: an exporter's revenue rises if, and only if, it still moves more than P₀/P₁ = 62.9% of its pre-war barrels. Above the line, the price gain outruns the volume loss. Below it, nothing saves you. Each exporter below is plotted twice — where its pipeline capacity said it should land, and where it actually landed.

Price and transit, January – July 2026

The shock, and the truce that didn't hold

Brent peaked at $116 on 30 March. The 17 June memorandum of understanding brought it back to pre-war levels within a fortnight. Then three merchant ships were struck on 7 July and transits collapsed again — the current $76.58 sits on a corridor that is still, functionally, shut.

The other side of the strait

Who is on the receiving end

89.4% of Hormuz crude goes to Asia. Exposure below combines how much of a country's crude arrives through the strait with how long its stockpile could replace those specific barrels. India scores near the top not because it is the most dependent, but because it holds 250 mb against China's 1,541.

Japan (74% of crude via Hormuz) and South Korea (70%) are more dependent than anything scored here. Neither publishes inventory data in this dataset, so both are excluded rather than imputed — the gap is left visible on purpose.

Method and limits

What this does and doesn't establish

Model

Revenue R = Q · P. Comparing the conflict window to the pre-war baseline, revenue rises iff Q₁/Q₀ > P₀/P₁. The right-hand side is the break-even retention rate r*. With P₀ = $64 (Feb 2026) and a time-weighted P₁ = $101.70 across 28 Feb – 8 May, r* = 62.9%.

Ex-ante retention is spare bypass ÷ Hormuz volume, capped at 1. Iran is set to 1 — it controlled the strait it was closing. Time-weighting matters: a three-day spike should not count the same as a five-week plateau.

Limits

  • Partial equilibrium. Price is treated as exogenous to any single exporter, which is a strong assumption for Saudi Arabia.
  • Revenue direction is directional only. The NYT reports signs for Gulf states, not magnitudes, so the test is a sign test on six observations — suggestive, not powered.
  • Output-cut estimates are trade-press figures, not audited production data.
  • Crude and condensate only. LNG (Qatar's force majeure) and refined products move on different economics and would change Qatar's picture.
  • Bypass "spare" capacity is the IEA's 3.5–5.5 mb/d range, midpoint-allocated. The UAE result is sensitive to this allocation — which is precisely the point.
