The Hormuz
break-even line

When the strait shut, every Gulf exporter lost barrels. Only some lost money. Saudi Arabia is the largest exporter through Hormuz and its oil revenue rose. The UAE is the third largest and its revenue fell. Production scale explains nothing about who survived. One variable, fixed years before the war, explains almost all of it.

The chokepoint, to scale

Fourteen million barrels a day, one aperture

Crude and condensate from seven exporters converge on a four-mile-wide shipping lane and fan out to Asia. Two countries own pipelines that reach open water without using it. Everyone else has no route out of the Gulf at all. Switch the state below to see what the closure physically did to the flow.

The model

Revenue is volume times price. Price did the rescuing.

Brent ran at 1.72× its pre-war level through the conflict window. That multiple sets a single threshold the whole market shares: an exporter's revenue rises if, and only if, it still moves more than P₀/P₁ ≈ 58% of its pre-war barrels. Above the line, the price gain outruns the volume loss. Below it, nothing saves you. Each exporter below is plotted twice — where its pipeline capacity said it should land, and where it actually landed.

The one miss is the finding. The UAE owns ADCOP, which on paper left it 76% retention — comfortably above the line. Its revenue fell 25% anyway (Goldman Sachs), an implied 44% realised retention: ADCOP moved ~2 mb/d in March, half its February throughput. Rated capacity is not deliverable capacity — EIA calls the UAE's real excess bypass only "limited," the same wedge that shrinks corridor-wide deliverable spare to 2.6 mb/d against a 4.9 mb/d nameplate sum. Any resilience estimate built off an infrastructure inventory will overstate what a corridor can actually absorb.

Price and transit, January – July 2026

The shock, and the truce that didn't hold

Brent crossed $100 on 8 March — its first time in four years — and peaked near $126. The closure came in phases, not one clean arc: a US naval blockade ran 13 April – 29 May, a memorandum was signed 17 June, and Iran re-closed the strait on 20 June. The latest ~$76.58 sits on a corridor that is still, functionally, contested.

The other side of the strait

Who is on the receiving end

84% of Hormuz crude goes to Asia (EIA). Exposure below combines how much of a country's crude arrives through the strait with how long its stockpile could replace those specific barrels. Japan (74% of crude via Hormuz) and South Korea (70%) are more dependent than anything scored here. Neither publishes inventory data in this dataset, so both are excluded rather than imputed — the gap is left visible on purpose.

Method and limits

What this does and doesn't establish

Model

Revenue R = Q · P. Comparing the conflict window to the pre-war baseline, revenue rises iff Q₁/Q₀ > P₀/P₁. The right-hand side is the break-even retention rate r*. With P₀ = $61 (the pre-conflict-risk baseline, before war premium) and a time-weighted P₁ = $104.73 across 28 Feb – 8 May, r* ≈ 58.2%.

Ex-ante retention is spare bypass ÷ Hormuz volume, capped at 1. Iran is set to 1 — it controlled the strait it was closing. Time-weighting matters: a three-day spike should not count the same as a five-week plateau.

Limits & how they're handled

  • Partial equilibrium — price is exogenous to any single exporter (strong for Saudi Arabia). Probed two ways: a ±15% sweep of P₁, and swapping the pre-war baseline P₀ from the counterfactual $61 to the immediate pre-strike $72. The zero-bypass losers and Iran are called under every assumption; only Saudi Arabia, sitting a few points from the line, flips — its narrow +10% gain would be mis-called on the $72 baseline. That fragility is shown, not hidden.
  • Small sample. Goldman Sachs / FREE Network published magnitudes for the key exporters, so the test is no longer sign-only: structural retention rank-orders the revenue moves (Spearman ρ = +0.74), not just their sign. Still six observations — suggestive, not powered.
  • Realised retention is derived from reported revenue (Q₁/Q₀ = revenue index ÷ price index), replacing the earlier guessed output cuts. Baseline caveat: the revenue window and the price baseline differ slightly, so implied retention is indicative.
  • Crude and condensate only. LNG (~20% of world seaborne LNG, and Qatar's force majeure) moves on different economics and is not modeled — it would change Qatar's picture.
  • Bypass spare uses EIA's real deliverable figure (2.6 mb/d combined), not a nameplate sum (4.9). Individual country shares beyond Saudi Arabia, daily prices between dated events, and importer inventories are flagged as estimates in each CSV, not presented as sourced fact.
